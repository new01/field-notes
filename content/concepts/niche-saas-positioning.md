Niche SaaS positioning is the deliberate strategy of building software for narrow, well-defined verticals rather than competing in crowded horizontal markets. Instead of building "project management for everyone," you build project management for veterinary clinics, or inventory tracking for independent grocery stores, or asset management for motion designers.

## Why niche positioning works

Broad markets attract broad competition. As AI lowers the barrier to shipping software, horizontal categories get flooded with near-identical products. The defense against this isn't building faster — it's building *more specifically*.

A niche product wins because:

- **Deep workflow fit** — the product maps exactly to how a specific group of professionals already work, rather than forcing them into a generic model
- **Domain-specific language** — the UI, documentation, and onboarding speak the user's language, not generic SaaS jargon
- **Smaller attack surface** — competitors building for "everyone" can't justify the effort to match your depth in a 500-person niche
- **Word-of-mouth density** — tight professional communities talk. One happy motion designer tells other motion designers

## The AI-era angle

AI amplifies the niche strategy in two ways. First, AI-powered development makes it economically viable to build products for markets that were previously too small to justify the engineering investment. A vertical that supports 200 paying customers becomes viable when your build cost drops by an order of magnitude.

Second, AI-driven market noise makes it *harder* for generalist products to stand out. When every broad-market product looks the same and makes the same claims, the product that clearly says "this is built for *you*" cuts through instantly.

## Picking the right niche

Not every narrow market is a good niche. Strong niches share a few characteristics:

- **Professionals with budget** — the target users have money to spend on tools (or their employers do)
- **Underserved workflows** — they're currently using generic tools with workarounds, spreadsheets, or manual processes
- **Community structure** — the vertical has forums, conferences, Slack groups, or associations where word travels
- **Recurring need** — the problem is ongoing, not a one-time fix

The worst niches are ones where users have no budget, no community, or where an existing tool already dominates with deep vertical integration.

## From niche to platform

A common growth path for niche SaaS is to start narrow, dominate a vertical, then expand to adjacent niches. The key discipline is resisting the urge to go broad too early. Each new vertical should feel like a deliberate expansion, not a dilution of focus.
